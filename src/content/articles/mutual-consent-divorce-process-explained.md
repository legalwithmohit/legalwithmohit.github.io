When both spouses agree that a marriage should end, the law provides a comparatively faster and less adversarial route than a contested divorce. It is still, however, a formal legal process with specific steps.

## Step 1: A joint petition

Both spouses jointly file a petition stating that they have been living separately for the required period, have been unable to live together, and have mutually agreed to dissolve the marriage. This is filed together, not by one party against the other.

## Step 2: The first motion

At the first hearing, the court records the statements of both spouses confirming their consent and examines the settlement terms covering maintenance, custody of any children, and division of property.

## Step 3: The cooling-off period

A waiting period generally follows before the case can proceed to its second stage, intended to give both parties time to reconsider. Courts can shorten or waive this period in appropriate circumstances.

## Step 4: The second motion

If both spouses still wish to proceed after the cooling-off period, they appear again to confirm their consent has not changed. If it has changed for either party, the petition does not proceed on a mutual consent basis and would need to follow a contested route instead.

## Step 5: The decree

Once the court is satisfied that consent is genuine, informed, and unchanged, it passes a decree of divorce, formally and legally ending the marriage.

## Realistic timelines

Uncomplicated matters, where the settlement is already agreed and paperwork is in order, often conclude within six months to a year and a half from filing. Delays typically come from unresolved disputes over maintenance or custody rather than from the court process itself — which is exactly why a clear, complete settlement before filing tends to save the most time.

## What if one spouse changes their mind?

Either spouse can withdraw consent at any point before the second motion is heard. If that happens, the mutual consent petition cannot proceed as filed, and the matter would need to be pursued, if at all, as a contested divorce on other legal grounds.
