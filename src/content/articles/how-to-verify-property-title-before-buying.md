Buying property is one of the largest financial decisions most people make, and the single biggest risk is not the price — it is discovering, after payment, that the seller did not have a clear right to sell.

## What "clear title" actually means

A clear title means the person selling the property genuinely owns it, has the legal right to transfer it, and there are no competing claims, unpaid loans, or pending court cases attached to it. Title problems rarely announce themselves. They surface later, often when you try to sell the property yourself or apply for a loan against it.

## Step 1: Trace the chain of ownership

Ask for the original sale deed and every subsequent transfer document, going back as far as practically possible — 30 years is a reasonable benchmark. Each transfer in the chain should be properly registered and internally consistent: names, survey numbers, and boundaries should match across documents.

## Step 2: Check the encumbrance certificate

This certificate, issued by the sub-registrar's office, lists any mortgages, liens, or legal claims registered against the property during a chosen period. A property with an active loan against it can still be sold, but only if that loan is cleared as part of the transaction — and that needs to be documented properly, not just promised verbally.

## Step 3: Confirm there is no ongoing litigation

A search at the relevant civil court, and increasingly through online case-status portals, can reveal whether the property is the subject of an active dispute. This step is easy to skip and one of the most important not to.

## Step 4: Verify mutation and revenue records

Mutation records reflect whose name the property is currently recorded under for the purposes of local tax and revenue. These should match the person you are buying from — a mismatch is a signal to investigate further before proceeding, not after.

## Step 5: Get a lawyer's opinion before you pay

A title search is not simply reading documents; it is knowing which discrepancies matter and which are routine. A written legal opinion on title, obtained before any payment changes hands, is the single most effective protection available to a buyer.

## What if a problem turns up after payment?

Options narrow considerably once money has changed hands, though they are not eliminated — remedies can include rescission of the sale, a claim for damages, or, in some cases, a suit for specific performance against the seller. All of these are harder, slower, and more expensive than catching the issue beforehand.
