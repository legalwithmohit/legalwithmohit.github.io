Online shopping disputes are one of the most common reasons people first look into consumer protection law, usually after a product arrives damaged, doesn't match its description, or a promised refund never comes through.

## Step 1: Document everything immediately

Before contacting anyone, save the product listing, your order confirmation, payment receipt, and photos or video of the product as received. This record matters far more once a dispute has started than it seems to at the time of the purchase.

## Step 2: Raise the issue with the seller in writing

A message through the platform's official channel, or an email, creates a timestamped record of when you raised the problem and what response you received. Phone calls are harder to prove later, so it's worth following up any call with a written summary.

## Step 3: Escalate to the platform if the seller doesn't respond

Most e-commerce platforms have a formal escalation or grievance process separate from the individual seller. Platforms can, in many circumstances, share responsibility for a transaction they facilitated.

## Step 4: Send a legal notice

If informal attempts don't resolve things within a reasonable time, a formal legal notice sets out the problem, what you are asking for, and a deadline to respond. This step alone resolves a significant number of disputes, since it signals the matter is being taken seriously.

## Step 5: File a consumer complaint

If the notice doesn't produce a resolution, a complaint can be filed with the appropriate consumer commission based on the value of the claim. The process is designed to be accessible without a lawyer, though legal help is useful for larger or more complex claims.

## What compensation can you realistically expect?

Consumer forums can order a refund, replacement, or repair, and in many cases additional compensation for the inconvenience caused and the cost of pursuing the complaint. The amount awarded generally reflects the actual loss and inconvenience shown, rather than a fixed penalty.
